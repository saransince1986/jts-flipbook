Getting to space: present, future, and fiction

Swipe to begin

Progress: a cargo carrier

This unpiloted Russian spacecraft, launched atop a Soyuz rocket, regularly carries food, equipment, and supplies—but not people—to the International Space Station.

Soyuz: a three-seater

The Russian Soyuz space capsule can carry three crewmembers to and from the Space Station.

ATV: Europe’s heavy hauler

Europe’s Automated Transfer Vehicle can carry more than 16,900 pounds of cargo into orbit—about three times Progress’s capacity. It launches on an Ariane 5 rocket.

Dragon: a private pioneer

The SpaceX Dragon capsule, launched on a Falcon 9 rocket, was the first commercial spacecraft to deliver cargo to the Space Station.

Cygnus: ISS resupply

Orbital Sciences’ cargo-carrying Cygnus spacecraft launches atop an Antares rocket. Under a NASA contract, orbital sciences will deliver at least 20 tons of cargo to the ISS.

H-II Transfer Vehicle: Japan’s bird

Designed, built, and launched in Japan, the HTV (also called Kounotori, or “white stork”) can deliver more than 12,000 pounds of cargo to the International Space Station.

Space elevator: cable climber

Space elevators would run capsules, or “climbers,” up and down cables reaching from Earth into space. A problem: the cables would need to be about 22,000 miles long.

Teleportation: beam me up

Star Trek-style teleportation may be possible theoretically, but the technology is still a long, long, long . . . long way in the future.

Mass driver: super catapult

A mass driver launcher would accelerate a magnetically levitated vehicle off the end of a ramp—sort of like a maglev train going off a jump into space. (Which would be awesome.)

Supplies from space

Producing supplies in space could be cheaper than launching them from Earth. In the future, we may mine materials from the moon, asteroids, or other planets.
