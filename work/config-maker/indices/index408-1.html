Preparing food for space

Swipe to begin

Not exactly mouthwatering

The earliest space food was extremely light and compact. But it wasn’t very appetizing.

Food cubes and tubes

Early astronauts munched bite-sized food cubes and squeezed purees into their mouths from toothpaste-type tubes.

Where no shrimp has gone before

Space food has improved over the years. A current mission favorite is shrimp cocktail.

Impressive shelf life

Space food is made to last. It needs to stay safe and healthy on months-long missions.

Prepping for space

We often freeze-dry veggies destined for space. Without any moisture, bacteria won’t grow on these vegetables.

The taste test

Astronauts taste-test food before missions. There are plenty of options to choose from, so it isn’t necessary for every astronaut to like every food.

A team effort

Food scientists develop space foods, nutritional biochemists determine nutritional requirements, and dieticians handle mission menu planning. Astronauts only have to eat it.

Plan ahead, stay fed

You can’t easily pack all the food you’d need for a mission that’d last several years. How can astronauts help keep their cupboards full?

Gardens in space

Scientists are experimenting with growing food crops on the International Space Station. An on-board garden could help feed astronauts on long missions.

Printing food?

Astronauts on future missions might “print” a wide range of foods. With nutritive powders and oils for “ink,” 3D printers could create anything from veggie burgers to chocolate bars.

Red planet greens

If humans one day colonize other planets, such as Mars, we could grow our food in greenhouses.
