Taking a space walk

The right stuff (and training)

Astronauts must train for hundreds of hours before they ever set foot (or float) outside their spacecraft.

Going under

Part of astronauts’ training takes place in a 6.2-million-gallon pool, where buoyancy simulates weightlessness. They practice moving in space suits, using tools, and dealing with emergency situations.

Something’s missing . . .

Why isn’t this astronaut floating? She’s training on Earth, wearing a practice space suit in a simulated airlock.

Ready for the real thing

In space, astronauts help each other into their suits. Getting dressed for a spacewalk takes about 45 minutes.

Under pressure

The air pressure inside space suits keeps astronauts alive in the vacuum of space. But to prevent the suits from becoming rigid, the pressure is kept low—much lower than inside spacecraft.

Beating the bends

Astronauts breathe pure oxygen while slowly adjusting to their space suits’ air pressure. This rids their bodies of nitrogen, which could form deadly bubbles in their blood (called “the bends”) in the suits’ lower pressure.

Double doors

Before these spacewalkers can open the hatch to space, their crewmates must close the inner door behind them and activate pumps to remove all the air from the airlock.

Hostile work environment

The airlock’s outer hatch opens, and the astronauts are in space! They’ll work among micrometeoroids, extreme temperature shifts, and cosmic radiation . . . just another day at the office.

A personal atmosphere

Space suits contain oxygen and provide the air pressure our bodies need. But that air pressure also makes the suit stiff and challenging to move in.

Staying attached

Drifting off into space would be very bad, so astronauts connect themselves to their spacecraft with tethers.

Staying in touch

When astronauts are on spacewalks, they stay in constant communication with their colleagues on the Space Station and with Mission Control on Earth.

Staying on task

During a space walk, every astronaut has a specific “to-do” list. They know exactly what needs to get done and how to do it.

A hard day’s work

There’s a lot of work to do on a spacewalk. Science experiments, repairs, and construction work keep spacewalking astronauts busy for up to eight hours.

Worth the effort

Working in a pressurized space suit is difficult and tiring, but astronauts consider it a huge honor. It’s an experience unlike anything else on—or off—Earth.
