Many arms

The International Space Station has several robotic arms and two large cranes. The 58-foot-long Canadarm2 is the Station’s largest robotic arm.

Docked by ‘bots

Astronauts on the Space Station use robotic arms to dock supply vehicles. Here, the Canadarm2 secures SpaceX’s Dragon capsule.

Astronaut transportation

Moving around on a spacewalk isn’t easy. Astronauts sometimes hitch rides on the Station’s robotic arms to get from one place to another.

A handy robot

Dextre, the Space Station’s robotic “hand,” can perform detailed tasks like changing batteries or replacing cameras.

Big in Canada

The Canadian five-dollar bill features the Canadarm2 and Dextre robots—both are products of the Canadian Space Agency.

Robonaut2

One way operators can control Robonaut2 is through telerobotics. A distant telerobotic operator wears a special visor, vest, and gloves to control Robonaut2 with body movements.

SPHERES

Call them “Synchronized Position Hold, Engage, Reorient Experimental Satellites” . . . or just “SPHERES.” These free-flying, bowling-ball-sized robots test autonomous spacecraft flight aboard the Space Station.

Curiosity Rover

Curiosity began exploring the surface of Mars in 2012. Robotic pioneers are exploring the solar system in advance of the first human visitors.

RATS and robots

Members of NASA’s Desert Research and Technology Studies, the Desert RATS, test new technologies in remote deserts. These vehicles could one day explore the Moon or Mars.

A walking mobile home

This towering, 6-legged robotic rover, called ATHLETE, is only a half-scale prototype. Future models could carry habitation modules across the Moon or Mars.

Robotic companions?

Robots will accompany astronauts and explorers wherever they go. Unfortunately, true robotic companions like C-3PO
and R2-D2 are still only a fantasy. Oh dear.
